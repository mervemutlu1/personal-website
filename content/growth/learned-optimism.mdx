Everyone wants happiness, an easy and enjoyable life. Unfortunately there is a lot we do not have control over. Sometimes unexpected events happen. These undesirable events don't always have to be big losses or devastating situations. Even very small things can harm us psychologically.

Some people can stand strong in devastating events. Their psychology is often little or not affected at all. And then there are people like me who can be hurt by a small incident and carry it for a long time.

For example, I can let a friend's word affect my whole week. A comment said with good intentions can make me feel terrible for days. So why can I be made unhappy so easily when there are people who can stand stronger in far worse situations?

I searched a lot for the answer to this question. In this article, I will tell you how to look at bad events differently, what learned optimism is, and how I approach things now.

---

The father of the concept of learned optimism is American psychologist Dr. Martin Seligman. He published a book of the same name. According to Seligman, it is necessary to strive for optimism and to see solutions, possibilities, and advantages in every situation encountered in life.

>> Being optimistic takes effort and willpower. You have to work at it. Which means it can be learned.

---

## 1. This too shall pass

When we encounter something bad, we focus on it completely. We think "my life is over, everything is ruined." But how we approach the situation affects us just as much as the situation itself.

We should try to focus on "this too shall pass" rather than the situation itself. You've probably been there before. It is not your first breakdown. You have been through hard things and you made it through. You can do it again.

I still try to change my mindset this way. Attaching this to your life isn't easy — especially when old traumas surface unexpectedly. Yet, with enough practice, everything gets better.

## 2. Mistakes belong where they are made

Don't put the weight of one failure on all parts of your life. If you failed a math exam, it doesn't mean you failed everything. One bad relationship doesn't mean all future relationships will go the same way.

Do not generalize. Think about why you are feeling bad, but try to be realistic. Are you feeling bad about a mistake, or are you letting it define your whole life?

I've been there. I felt like a failure just because I chose the wrong career. Now I'm accepting that my career is just one part of my life. It doesn't define me.

## 3. Mistakes don't define your personality

Sometimes we take risks and things don't work out. But the reason things went wrong isn't always you. There are situations outside your control.

Two startups with the same aim can end up very differently even doing everything the same. The world is changing. Sometimes you lose because it wasn't the right time — not because of who you are.

>> Accept your success. If you want to be happy, stop attributing your wins to luck. Your achievements come from you.

---

According to Seligman's theory of learned optimism, the skills needed to get rid of helplessness can be learned. It is believing that there is a way to succeed, and seeing failures as temporary rather than permanent. It is developing solutions and moving forward against the problems you face.

I hope you get the happiness you want and seek — and more importantly, that you stay there.
